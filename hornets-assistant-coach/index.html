Hornets assistant coach believes Boston will love Kemba Walker

admin | July 9, 2019 | Analysis, NBA Free Agency, News | No Comments

Boston Celtics point guard Kemba Walker hasn’t been painted as the savior of Boston’s hope at contention, which is more ideal for a team that fell victim to the pressure of high expectations last season than to project them as championship-contenders so early on.

However, Walker’s personality and demeanor has been compared to former Celtics point guard Kyrie Irving’s at nearly every turn, as mercurial guard’s personality and attitude rubbed a lot of people the wrong way last season. In fact, the Celtics front office appeared to be set on adding players with personal qualities that were the antithesis of Irving’s.

Like Celtics superfan Grant Williams, a player who is perpetually ecstatic about playing for Boston. Or like rookie point guard Tremont Waters, a player whose excitement is palpable, and seems to be bouncing with joy every time you see him.

Or like Walker, whose selflessness, humility and agreeable attitude have been highlighted since news broke that he and the Celtics had mutual interest in one another.

Per the Boston Globe’s Adam Himmelsbach, Charlotte Hornets assistant coach Ronald Nored believes that Walker — who spent the first eight seasons of his career with the Hornets — is “a guy that people in Boston will love. They’ll be able to wrap their arms him… People will love it. And people will like him because of the kind of person he is and the way he plays.”

Further, where there was friction between Irving and Celtics head coach Brad Stevens, Nored says that “Kemba is super unselfish, and he’s humble … I think that fits with Brad perfectly.”

“There’s going to be no issues there. It’s going to be seamless, because they’re two like-minded people that push other people to get better, and they’re good people. It’s going to work so well and I think they’re going to have a great relationship.”

Walker’s character has been talked up more than his on-court ability to be frank, and though it’s bordering on excessive, it’s understandable. Irving’s passive-aggressiveness didn’t single-handedly destroy Boston’s chances of winning a championship but it was far from ideal from what the Celtics needed from their leader.

Togetherness and chemistry was an issue for Boston throughout the season and their disconnectedness off-the-court would be reflected on it. This season, that doesn’t appear as if it will be the Celtics’ issue at all.

In fact, if they were to fail to make a deep playoff run, it would be because they weren’t strong enough defensively — as Walker and Enes Kanter are two players with a reputation for playing poor defense and may be targeted heavily in pick-and-rolls or switches — or talented enough. The latter scenario would be caused by stagnation or regression by Celtics young wings Jaylen Brown and Jayson Tatum, who the team is relying on heavily this season.

A fiery leader who leaves it all on the court every time he plays but one with a rather sensational style of play, Walker is in prime position to win over the hearts of a fanbase and franchise that was jilted at the altar when Irving opted to focus on signing with the Brooklyn Nets.

At this point, it’s well-known that the Celtics have brought in a high-character point guard. Now, Walker just needs to prove his worth to the organization, on and off the court.
